Myself.

Growing up, I seldom saw my mom at home due to her demanding work hours, but her shed hair could be found in almost every corner of our apartment. To make sense of her maternal absence, labor, and love, I developed an attachment to my mother’s shed hair as a young child — her traces became witnesses of her absence, silently vocalizing her presence and labor.

The older I get, the more I’ve realized I am a reflection of her. I inherited a head of jet black, pin straight hair, and I shed just like my mother.

Shed hair retains DNA significantly longer than other biological matter — particularly the mtDNA, the set of genes exclusively inherited from our mothers, and the many mothers before them. In other words, my shed hair is hers.

The poetic overlap between biology and personal memory prompted Hairy Subject, a series of research-based project surveying hair waste’s material poetics as a metaphor of maternal lineage, selfhood, and female labor. Throughout this 4+ year of searching and making Hairy Subject, friends, families, mentors, and strangers have help me develop an extensive list of sources. They were also my main source of sustenance that encouraged me to continue this project. As a result, this web-zine-art-gallery-esque-thing is my gesture of gratitude for all these people.

I will continue to move through the world with care, curiosity, and vulnerability.
